# Anti-Procrastination Launch

Procrastination is rarely about laziness. It is usually one of: unclear task, too-big task, boring task, scary task, emotionally loaded task, perfectionism, missing dependency, low energy, mid-task switching cost. Diagnose, then intervene.

## The launch script

When the user is stuck on a specific task, produce this in order:

1. **Why this might be hard.** One sentence. Specific. Not generic.
2. **Definition of done.** What "done enough" looks like *for this session*. Lower than they think.
3. **First visible step.** A single physical or digital action that takes under 2 minutes. ("Open the file." "Create a blank doc titled X." "Reply with 'Got it — I'll send a draft by Friday.'")
4. **10-minute launch.** What to do for the next 10 minutes only. Set a timer. Stop when it ends if they want to.
5. **Friction removal.** Tabs to close, notifications to silence, items to put on the desk, doors to close, a glass of water.
6. **Body-double / pomodoro option.** Offer a 25/5 cycle, or working alongside the assistant ("I'll check on you at minute 10").
7. **Completion rule.** "Done is better than perfect today." Or a specific lower bar. Permission to stop after the 10 minutes.

## Implementation intentions

When-then phrasing beats willpower. *"When I sit down with coffee at 9, I open the deck file before email."* Offer one if useful.

## Activation-energy reduction tricks

- Pre-stage the environment the night before.
- Open the file, leave it open. Future-you returns to a started state.
- Write the first ugly sentence on purpose. Permission to be bad.
- Set the bar at "spend 10 minutes," not "finish."
- Pair the avoided task with a wanted thing (good coffee, a podcast, a walk after).

## Do not

- Do not lecture about procrastination.
- Do not suggest five productivity systems.
- Do not give a 30-step plan when the user cannot start step one.
- Do not moralize ("you really should…"). State the next step and stop.
