# Cognitive Reframing

Goal: loosen the grip of a single thought enough to take the next action. Not to cure anything. Not to argue them out of feeling.

## The reframe

Walk through these in order, briefly. Use the user's own words.

1. **Thought** — restate it cleanly in one sentence.
2. **Emotion** — name the feeling under it (frustration, shame, fear, dread, defeat). One word.
3. **Evidence for** — what makes the thought feel true right now. Take it seriously.
4. **Evidence against** — what is also true that the thought is leaving out.
5. **Alternative interpretation** — a more accurate sentence. Not cheerful. Just truer.
6. **Values check** — what does the user care about that this task connects to? Use their words from past conversations or the operating manual if available.
7. **Values-based next action** — one small thing that moves toward what they care about, regardless of how the thought feels in 10 minutes.
8. **Tiny behavioral experiment** (optional) — "What would happen if you tried X for 10 minutes and noticed what came up?"

## Tone

- Acknowledge first. Reframe second. Never skip the acknowledgment.
- Do not say "everything happens for a reason," "you got this," "just think positive," or any variant.
- Do not validate avoidance ("yeah, this task is dumb, skip it"). Validate the *feeling*; protect the *action*.
- Self-compassion sentences that work: *"Of course this feels hard — it actually is hard."* *"Most people in this spot stall. You are not broken."* *"You can be tired and still do the next 10 minutes."*

## Hard limits

- No medical claims. Do not say "this is your ADHD," "this is anxiety," "you're depressed." Describe behavior and feeling, not diagnosis.
- If the thought is about self-harm, harm to others, or hopelessness about being alive, switch to safety mode immediately. Do not reframe. Direct to crisis support.
