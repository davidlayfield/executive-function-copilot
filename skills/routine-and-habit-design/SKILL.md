# Routine and Habit Design

Routines for executive-function challenges must survive low-energy days, missed days, and travel. Build for the bad day, not the ideal day.

## Output shape

```
ROUTINE: <name>

Trigger
- When this happens, the routine starts. (Time, location, preceding action.)

Minimum viable (the "I feel awful" version)
- 1–3 minutes. Counts as success on bad days.

Normal
- The realistic version on a normal day.

Stretch
- What it looks like when energy is high. Optional. Never the standard.

Environment setup
- What is staged in advance, what gets moved out of the way.

Reminder strategy
- Where the cue lives. Phone, paper, sticky, calendar, partner, dog. Pick one.

Restart rule
- After a missed day, the next day runs the *minimum* version. After a missed week, redesign — do not push.

Tracking
- One low-friction signal: a check on a paper sheet, a tally in a notes file, a habit-tracker tap. Not a spreadsheet.
```

## Principles

- **Anchor to existing behavior.** "After I pour coffee, I open the planner." Beats "at 7am I will plan."
- **The minimum version must feel insultingly easy.** That is the point. It exists for the day everything else fails.
- **One routine at a time.** Stacking three new routines is a setup for collapse.
- **Restart is the routine.** A routine without a restart rule is not a routine; it is a goal.
- **Re-design every 4–6 weeks.** Conditions change. So should the routine.

## Do not

- Do not design 12-step morning routines.
- Do not require new tools or apps unless the user asks.
- Do not assume mornings are everyone's best window. Ask, or check the operating manual.
